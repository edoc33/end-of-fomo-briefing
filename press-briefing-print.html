Search answers once. The web keeps changing.

AI search answers “what’s true now?” Prices change. Deadlines pass. Opportunities close while you’re looking elsewhere. Information agents keep watching after the search ends.

Why this category is becoming real

Only a small slice of the web needs watching.
That slice is now a market.

The supply side
< 1%
of web pages change within a week.
Most of those changes are noise: ad rotations, timestamps, layout tweaks. The smaller subset users want alerts about (prices, policies, job postings, regulatory filings, status pages, court dockets) is precisely where missing a change is expensive.
Source: Visualping
The demand side
1
Google just named it.
I/O 2026 gave the behavior a consumer name: “information agents.” That gives reporters a clean category to explain.
2
Money is moving.
Exa raised $250M at a $2.2B valuation (May 20, 2026, led by Andreessen Horowitz). OpenAI bundled ChatGPT Pulse into Pro. Investors see a market.
3
The demand is already visible.
Visualping AI processed 16.8M page checks in April 2026. Our binary IMPORTANT classifier flagged 1 in 5 detected changes; the rest is filtered as noise. ChatGPT Pulse is a Pro feature. Consumers already pay for proactive monitoring; they just call it different names.

Open-web agent · in practice

From a standing question to a notification on the lock screen.

1. Set up: ask the agent
A standing request typed into Search: “keep me updated when any of my favorite athletes announce sneaker collabs.” The agent goes off and watches.
2. Alert: lock-screen push
The agent pings you the moment something matches, synthesized from the open web, delivered as a push.
Page monitoring agent · in practice

From a URL to a precise change alert.

1. Set up — on the page
The user picks the exact page and tells Visualping what to watch for — in this case, “size 7 is back in stock.”
2. Alert — email with the exact change
The alert arrives with the precise diff highlighted on the page — “Out of stock” is now “Add to bag.”
Angles to pitch around this news

Five angles. Pick the one that fits your beat.

01
“The I/O announcement most recaps rushed past.”
Information agents got buried under AI Mode coverage. Tell the story most recaps skipped: what they are, why they matter, and why readers missed them.
02
“A category Google just named.”
Trace the lineage: Google Alerts (2003) → Visualping (2017) → ChatGPT Pulse (2025) → Exa $250M (May 2026) → Google rollout (Summer 2026).
03
“You don’t have to wait for Google.”
Google’s agents arrive this summer for Pro/Ultra subscribers only. Several alternatives, including Visualping, are already free or low-cost and consumer-ready.
04
“Open-web agents vs. page monitoring agents.”
An explainer on two architectures: discovery (Google, Exa, ChatGPT Pulse, Perplexity) and precision (Visualping). Most readers will want both, but the distinction is still hard to see.
05
“A Vancouver SaaS built the consumer version in 2017.”
Visualping shipped the page monitoring agent version of what Google is now naming “information agents.” 16.8M monthly page checks. 1 in 5 changes flagged important. A small-company vs. Big Tech angle that holds up.
